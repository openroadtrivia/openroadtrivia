The Arrival

Where the Road Begins

You smell the lake before you see it. Driving in from the west on Ogden Avenue — the old Route 66 alignment — the first thing that hits you is Lake Michigan, cold and enormous, pushing weather across the whole South Side. The second thing you notice is the noise. Chicago doesn't ease you in. It starts at full volume.

The official eastern terminus of Route 66 sits at the intersection of Adams Street and Michigan Avenue, in the shadow of the Art Institute's bronze lions. Stand there and look west. In front of you: 2,448 miles of American road running all the way to the Santa Monica Pier. Behind you: the lake, the wind, and a city of 2.7 million people who have opinions about everything, including whether the deep dish at Lou Malnati's or Giordano's is the real thing.

Chicago is not a Route 66 city the way Tucumcari or Shamrock are Route 66 cities. It doesn't need the road. It never did. Route 66 needs Chicago — needed its stockyards, its rail yards, its manufacturing floor, and eventually its departing thousands who drove that highway south and west looking for somewhere else. The road begins here because this is where everything came from.

The families who pull up to that Adams Street sign will have different reactions depending on what they've read and who they are. Some will want a photo at the bronze plaque. Some will wonder why the road starts in a canyon of glass and steel. One kid will ask why there's no road here. Another will notice the lake and forget the sign entirely. All of them are right.

The Story

Before the Road, During the Road, After the Road

Act I

Before Route 66

Chicago was a Potawatomi trading village before it was anything else. The name itself comes from the Potawatomi word for the wild garlic that grew along the river — shikaakwa. Fort Dearborn went up in 1803. The city incorporated in 1837. By 1890, barely fifty years later, it was the fifth-largest city on earth. The meatpacking industry, the grain elevators, the railroads — Chicago was the machine that processed the American interior and shipped it east.

Act II

The Route 66 Years

When Route 66 was designated in 1926 — championed in part by Chicagoan Cyrus Avery — it made the city the official gateway to the American West. For two decades, the highway carried tourists, truckers, and the displaced. During the Great Migration, it carried something more significant: hundreds of thousands of Black Southerners who followed the road north to Chicago's South Side, transforming the city's culture, music, and politics in ways still visible today. The blues didn't invent Chicago. But the road brought the blues north, and Chicago turned it electric.

Act III

After the Bypass

When I-55 and I-90 drew their straight lines through Illinois in the 1950s and '60s, Chicago didn't notice much. It was too big to suffer the way Tucumcari suffered. The city kept growing, sprawling, arguing with itself. What Route 66 left behind in Chicago isn't a ghost town. It's a layer — a stratum of mid-century architecture, diners, signage, and neighborhood memory that sits underneath the city's present tense. You have to know where to look.

The Migration That Changed Everything

The Green Book — the travel guide for Black Americans navigating a segregated country — listed dozens of Chicago businesses as safe stops for travelers heading north. The Regal Theater on 47th Street. The Sutherland Hotel. The restaurants and barbers and beauty parlors of Bronzeville. The Great Migration brought roughly 500,000 Black Southerners to Chicago between 1910 and 1940. Route 66 was their road north. The South Side became their destination — and from that migration came Muddy Waters, Howlin' Wolf, Nat King Cole, and eventually the entire architecture of modern popular music.

The other history that doesn't show up on the tourist signs: Chicago's Polish, Czech, Italian, Jewish, Chinese, and Mexican communities all built the city alongside its Anglo founders — and often did it from neighborhoods they weren't allowed to leave. Chinatown, which you'll pass on the way to the lakefront, has been in roughly its current location since the 1920s, when racial covenants in other neighborhoods made it the only place Chinese families could legally buy property.

The Route Through Town

Street by Street

The Route 66 alignment through Chicago has shifted over the decades. The most historically significant and drivable route today follows this path — and it rewards anyone willing to park the car and use their feet for a few blocks.

The Alignment — Adams St. to Ogden Ave.

Michigan Ave. & Adams The official start. Park in Millennium Park garage (cheapest legal option near the terminus). Walk to the Route 66 sign on Adams. The Art Institute is directly to your north — if you only have one hour, spend it here.
Jackson Blvd. Heading west from Michigan, Jackson takes you under the L tracks through the Loop. The overhead train noise is part of the experience — it's been making that noise since 1897. At rush hour, the screeching on the curves is operatic.
Ogden Avenue The diagonal that cuts southwest across the grid is old Chicago — it follows an ancient Indigenous trail. Here the city shifts from downtown to neighborhood. The architecture changes. The people change. Pay attention. This is where Route 66 actually begins to feel like a road.
Cicero & Berwyn Working-class Chicago. Mexican restaurants, Polish bakeries, Vietnamese nail salons sharing a streetscape built in 1920. Park and walk if it's a weekend morning. The tamale spot two blocks off Ogden doesn't have a Yelp page. You'll smell it first.

Parking tip: The Millennium Park garage on Columbus Drive is your best bet for the Adams St. terminus — about $26/day but validated discounts exist. For Ogden Ave. exploration, street parking opens up quickly west of Ashland. Do not attempt to park on Michigan Avenue. Just don't.

Discovery Stops

The Stops Worth Planning Your Day Around

Chicago has more world-class cultural institutions per square mile than almost any American city. The ones below are the ones that earn their spots — and each has something most visitors never find.

📷
Field Museum — Natural History
/images/stops/field-museum-chicago.jpg
Discovery

The Field Museum

Sue the T. rex lives here — 67 million years old, 13 feet tall, and the most complete Tyrannosaurus skeleton ever found. The museum bought her at auction in 1997 for $8.36 million. But Sue is now in the main hall; the real gems are downstairs in the Egyptian collection and the Pawnee Earth Lodge, a full-scale reconstruction you can walk into.

Insider Tip Most people never find the Grainger Hall of Gems in the basement. The Hope Diamond gets the press, but the field-collected specimens here are extraordinary — and you'll have them mostly to yourself.
🕐 9am–5pm daily 💵 $30 adults / $15 kids ⏱ 2–3 hours
📷
Art Institute of Chicago
/images/stops/art-institute-chicago.jpg
Discovery

Art Institute of Chicago

Seurat's A Sunday on La Grande Jatte is here — you've seen it a hundred times in reproduction, but standing in front of it, the scale surprises you. It's enormous. It's also directly across the room from Grant Wood's American Gothic, which is much smaller than you expect. The juxtaposition is intentional and perfect.

Insider Tip The Thorne Miniature Rooms on the lower level — 68 precisely scaled rooms from European and American history — are inexplicably moving. Kids are transfixed. Adults get quiet. Budget 30 minutes you didn't plan on.
🕐 11am–5pm (Thu to 8pm) 💵 $35 adults / free under 14 ⏱ 2–4 hours
📷
Chicago Riverwalk Architecture
/images/stops/chicago-riverwalk.jpg
Discovery

Chicago Architecture — The Riverwalk

Chicago is the city where modern architecture was invented. After the Great Fire of 1871 leveled 17,000 buildings, the city rebuilt itself in steel and glass — and in the process created the skyscraper. The Riverwalk architecture boat tour is the single best $50 you can spend in Chicago. You'll learn more about American history from the buildings than from any museum.

Insider Tip The free walking version of the architecture tour starts at the Chicago Architecture Center on Michigan Avenue. It's 90 minutes and rivals the boat tour for the blocks around the river. The volunteer docents are occasionally retired architects with feuds to settle about specific buildings.
🕐 Tours daily May–Oct 💵 $48–58 boat / free walking ⏱ 90 minutes
📷
Navy Pier — Lake Michigan
/images/stops/navy-pier-chicago.jpg
Discovery

Navy Pier & Lake Michigan

This is the thing about Navy Pier: the pier itself is fine, but Lake Michigan is extraordinary. Walk to the end. In July it smells like cold iron and sunscreen. In October it smells like the end of something. The lake is so large it has its own weather systems, its own tides, its own moods. Three hundred ships have sunk in it. It's not decorative water. It's inland ocean.

Insider Tip Skip the Ferris wheel and walk south along the lakefront path toward the Museum Campus. The view of the skyline from the lake path at dawn — around 6am in summer — is better than anything you'll see from a paid observation deck.
🕐 Open daily, free access 💵 Free (attractions vary) ⏱ 1–2 hours
📷
Millennium Park — The Bean
/images/stops/millennium-park-bean.jpg
Attraction

Millennium Park — Cloud Gate

"The Bean" arrived in 2006 and immediately became Chicago's most photographed object — which locals first resented, then accepted, then quietly came to love. The sculptor Anish Kapoor calls it Cloud Gate. Nobody calls it that. The underside, when you walk beneath it, distorts the skyline into something strange and vertiginous. Every photograph of it is the same. Every experience of it is different.

Insider Tip The Jay Pritzker Pavilion — Frank Gehry's bandshell in the park — hosts free concerts all summer. The Chicago Symphony performs outdoors in June. Getting there at 7pm on a Thursday with a bottle of wine from the Mariano's on Michigan costs nothing and is better than most things money can buy in this city.
🕐 6am–11pm daily 💵 Free ⏱ 30–60 minutes
📷
Museum of Science and Industry
/images/stops/msi-chicago.jpg
Discovery

Museum of Science and Industry

The MSI lives in Hyde Park in the former Palace of Fine Arts from the 1893 World's Columbian Exposition — the same fair that gave Chicago the Ferris wheel, the zipper, the dishwasher, and serial killer H.H. Holmes. Inside: a real German U-505 submarine captured in 1944, a coal mine you can ride down into, and a weather demonstration that involves a human-made tornado. It's the most exhaustingly good children's museum in America.

Insider Tip Book the U-505 submarine tour separately — it's an additional $18 but you get to go inside and stand in the actual torpedo room. The history of how the Navy captured a working U-boat in the middle of the Atlantic is one of World War II's better stories.
🕐 9:30am–4pm daily 💵 $25 adults / $16 kids ⏱ 3–5 hours
Attractions

Quick Stops Worth the Detour

01

Wrigley Field

Built in 1914, ivy on the outfield walls since 1937, the Cubs haven't permanently left even in their worst decades. The neighborhood around it — Wrigleyville — is loud and slightly desperate on game days and absolutely worth avoiding then. Go instead on a Tuesday morning in April when they've just opened for a weekday day game. The stadium is better when it's three-quarters full and the bleachers smell like spilled Old Style.

02

The Chicago Cultural Center

Free. Spectacular. Almost never crowded. The former Chicago Public Library (1897) has two Tiffany glass domes — Preston Bradley Hall's is 38 feet across and one of the most beautiful rooms in the Midwest. Admission: $0. The docents who explain it: priceless and slightly obsessive about the provenance of individual glass tiles.

03

Pilsen Murals — 18th Street

Pilsen is Chicago's Mexican American neighborhood — and one of the most mural-dense urban environments in the country. Walk west on 18th Street from Halsted. The National Museum of Mexican Art is here (free, world-class collection). The murals outside it are just as good. The tamales from the cart on the corner of 18th and Halsted on weekend mornings are better than both.

04

Graceland Cemetery — Architectural Pilgrimage

The architects who built Chicago are buried here: Louis Sullivan, Daniel Burnham, Ludwig Mies van der Rohe, Bruce Goff. Sullivan's own grave marker is considered a masterwork of Prairie School design. The cemetery itself is landscaped by Ossian Cole Simonds and functions as a quiet arboretum. Most visitors to Chicago have never heard of this place. That is your advantage.

Where to Stay

Four Nights, Four Different Chicagos

The Historic Option

The Palmer House Hilton

Built in 1871, destroyed in the Great Fire twelve days later, rebuilt bigger — the Palmer House has been running continuously since 1875 and is the longest-operating hotel in American history. The lobby ceiling is an 1882 painting. The brownie was supposedly invented here in 1893 for the World's Fair. The lobby bar is worth a drink even if you're not staying.

Request a room above the 8th floor on the south side for the best light. Get the brownie.
The Budget Pick

HI Chicago Hostel — South Loop

Sixteen blocks from the Route 66 terminus, private rooms available, clean and reliably staffed. The common areas attract international travelers who've done this trip before — the bulletin board in the lobby is a real-time guide to what's working on Route 66 right now. Cheaper than anything comparable within walking distance of the lake.

Book 3 weeks out in summer. Take the private room — worth the upgrade.
The Splurge

The Langham Chicago

Inside Mies van der Rohe's IBM Building on the river, which is itself a masterpiece of modernist architecture. The rooms face the river or the skyline. The pool is 52 feet long and sits on the 13th floor. The service is the kind that remembers what you ordered yesterday. If you're doing Route 66 once in your life and you want to start it right, start it here.

Ask for a Chicago River view room on a high floor. Sunset hits the water towers across the river at exactly 7:40pm in July.
What a Local Would Pick

The Robey — Wicker Park

A converted 1929 art deco clock tower in one of Chicago's most alive neighborhoods. Wicker Park has the record shops, the cocktail bars, the brunch spots, and the argument about gentrification all happening simultaneously within four blocks. The rooftop bar looks back at the downtown skyline. You're twenty minutes from the lake by L and feel entirely out of the tourist circuit.

Take the Blue Line from O'Hare directly to Damen stop. Walk 4 minutes. Done.
Where to Eat

Stop Asking About the Pizza. Start with the Italian Beef.

Every food writer who comes to Chicago spends half their time adjudicating the deep dish debate (Lou Malnati's wins, but Pequod's caramelized crust is the more interesting thing) and misses the actual argument: Chicago's Italian beef sandwich is one of the great American foods and nobody outside Illinois talks about it enough.

Breakfast

Yolk — Multiple Locations

The local chain that operates on Chicago time — fast, generous, and built for people with somewhere to be. The egg white frittata is the order if you're staying healthy. The French toast is the order if you're not. Lines at the River North location on weekends are genuine but move quickly. Worth it on your first morning before the museum run.

Order: The stuffed French toast or the Chicago scramble.
Lunch Counter

Al's #1 Beef — Little Italy

Since 1938. The Italian beef sandwich at Al's is served one of three ways: dry, wet, or dipped (fully submerged in au jus). Order it dipped with sweet peppers and hot giardiniera. Accept that you will destroy your shirt. This is the cost of admission. The location on Taylor Street in Little Italy is the original. Don't go to the airport location. Don't.

Order: Italian beef, dipped, hot giardiniera. Nothing else.
The Deep Dish Argument

Lou Malnati's — River North

If you must have deep dish — and you probably should, once — Lou Malnati's is the honest answer. The butter crust, the chunky tomatoes on top, the sausage patty rather than crumbles. Order it online before you arrive because the wait is 45 minutes otherwise, and you have museums to get to.

Order: The Lou — sausage, spinach, mushroom. One slice is a meal.
Dinner

Monteverde — West Loop

Chef Sarah Grueneberg's Italian-American restaurant in the West Loop restaurant corridor is the best pasta in a city with a lot of very good pasta. The Cacio e Pepe en Vesica — made tableside — is a performance worth watching. Not cheap. Not casual. Worth it for the night before you hit the road, when you want one last real meal before Joliet.

Order: The tableside cacio e pepe and whatever seasonal pasta they're pushing.
The One Nobody Mentions

Lem's Bar-B-Q — South Side

Lem's has been on 75th Street since 1954. The aquarium-smoked ribs — developed by the Lemmon family and never adequately replicated anywhere — are some of the best barbecue in the Midwest. No atmosphere. Styrofoam containers. A line that forms before they open. The South Side will teach you more about Chicago in one block than the Magnificent Mile will in three days.

Order: The rib tip combo and mild sauce on the side.
Late Night

The Wieners Circle — Lincoln Park

The Chicago hot dog is a religion: beef frank, yellow mustard, relish, onion, tomato, pickle spear, sport peppers, and a dash of celery salt, on a poppy seed bun. Never ketchup. The Wieners Circle is a late-night institution where the staff's spectacular rudeness is half the draw. Go after 11pm on a weekend. Go with a sense of humor about yourself.

Order: The char dog. Accept the attitude as part of the meal.
Where to Drink

The Old Bar, the Good Bar, and the Bar Locals Actually Go To

The Green Mill — Uptown

Al Capone's preferred jazz club since the 1920s. The booth he liked — where he could see both doors — is still there. Jazz seven nights a week, no cover on weeknights. The Green Mill is not a tourist trap dressed as a speakeasy. It is an actual jazz club that has been running jazz for a hundred years. The Uptown neighborhood around it tells a different Chicago story — Polish, Assyrian, Vietnamese — than the tourist circuit does.

The Violet Hour — Wicker Park

The bar that launched Chicago's craft cocktail scene in 2007 and has never stopped earning its reputation. No sign outside. Heavy velvet curtains. Cocktail menu written in complete sentences. This is where the serious drinkers go. Order something with rye whiskey. Trust the bartender. Don't ask for a Moscow Mule.

Schaller's Pump — Bridgeport

Opened in 1881. The oldest bar in Chicago still operating in its original location. Bridgeport is Daley country — four Chicago mayors came from this neighborhood — and Schaller's is where the political deals got made in the back booth over draft Old Style. It's a dive bar in the technical sense, meaning the drinks are cheap and unpretentious and the regulars have been sitting in those same spots since 1987.

Early Morning Coffee

Intelligentsia Coffee, which started in Chicago in 1995 and essentially taught the American coffee industry what direct trade meant, has locations throughout the city. The Millennium Park Coffeebar opens at 6:30am and is full of runners, architects, and dog walkers who want to be left alone with their oat lattes. Let them. Order your coffee and watch the park wake up.

Local Secrets

The Things the Tourist Board Forgot to Mention

  • Go to the Art Institute at 4:30pm on a Thursday Thursday is late night — the museum stays open until 8pm. By 5pm, the school groups are gone and the Impressionist galleries are as quiet as they get. Seurat's large painting in an empty room at late afternoon light is something you will think about for a while afterward.
  • The 606 Trail — a city within the city The 606 is an elevated rail trail that cuts through Wicker Park, Bucktown, Logan Square, and Humboldt Park. Walk it on a Saturday morning and you'll see: Ukrainian grandmothers pushing wire carts, murals from five different artistic traditions, a farmer's market at Milwaukee Avenue, the actual neighborhood arguments about who gets to live there. It is the best free 2.7 miles in Chicago.
  • The Chicago Riverwalk at 7am Before the lunch crowds arrive, the Riverwalk is populated by kayakers, dog walkers, and the occasional architect who wants to look at Mies's IBM Building from the water. The coffee stands open at 7. The river smells like clean stone in the morning. The buildings reflect in it in ways that require a photograph to confirm later.
  • Rain emergency: The Harold Washington Library Chicago's main public library (1991, the largest public library building in the world when it opened) has a reading room on the 9th floor with 20-foot arched windows looking south over the Loop. Free. Warm. Full of people actually reading. If it rains and you have nowhere to be, go here. Bring something to read.
  • Hyde Park — the neighborhood that isn't on the tour Twenty minutes south on the Metra from Millennium Station: the University of Chicago campus (Gothic stone, the size of a medieval town), the Museum of Science and Industry, Promontory Point (the best lake view in the city, free, no crowds), and the Medici — a coffee shop on 57th Street that has been the same coffee shop for 60 years and is the only place you'll hear an argument about Heidegger at 10am on a Tuesday.

Practical Info

Getting Here, Getting Around, Getting Out

Getting There

Chicago is the Route 66 starting point — there's no previous city on the route. O'Hare (ORD) is one of the world's busiest airports, 45 minutes from downtown by Blue Line CTA ($2.50). Midway (MDW) on the Southwest side is closer to some Route 66 neighborhoods. Amtrak's Union Station is where old Route 66 travelers arrived by rail.

Getting Around

The L covers most stops you'll want to hit. A 3-day CTA pass ($20) handles the lakefront museums, Wicker Park, and Hyde Park. Rent a car only if you're exploring neighborhoods off the main lines or starting your Route 66 drive. Ride-shares are fast but Chicago traffic is not — add 30 minutes to any app estimate during rush hour.

Best Season

June and September are perfect: warm, not oppressive, festivals running, lakefront accessible. July is hot and crowded and glorious if you're prepared. August has beach weather but serious humidity. October has arguably the best light in the city — clear, low, angled across the water. December through February: the wind off Lake Michigan at Michigan Avenue is legitimately dangerous. Come anyway if you can stand it.

How Long to Spend

Minimum: 2 days (lakefront and one neighborhood). Ideal: 4–5 days to actually feel the city. Chicago is not a place you can do in a day. It's also not a place that rewards rushing. The city moves at its own pace and rewards people who slow down to match it.

Emergency Info

Northwestern Memorial Hospital: 251 E. Huron St. — Level I trauma center, one of the best in the Midwest. Chicago non-emergency police: 311. The city's 311 system also handles noise complaints, pothole reports, and lost property — it's genuinely useful. Cell coverage: excellent throughout downtown and most neighborhoods. Can drop in underground L stations.

Next City on Route 66

From Adams & Michigan, drive west on Adams to Ogden Avenue, then southwest. You'll hit Joliet, Illinois in about 35 miles — 45 minutes if traffic cooperates, 75 if it doesn't. Welcome to Route 66. It only gets more interesting from here.
